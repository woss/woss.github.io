## Job role

I joined Ipsos Simstore as a Senior DevOps engineer and grew into the Platform Architect and AI Adoption Lead — designing infrastructure, developer tooling, and AI adoption strategy that shapes how the entire engineering team works. I own the cloud infrastructure strategy, developer experience, and platform reliability across a multi-region, highly available Kubernetes cluster.

I designed and built the service mesh architecture based on Kafka and Knative, enabling event-driven communication with automatic scaling — replacing a monolithic deployment model and cutting deployment times by orders of magnitude. Every piece of infrastructure is defined as code: Terragrunt, and OpenTofu across AWS environments.

As a platform engineer, my focus is developer enablement: CI/CD pipelines, monitoring and observability, security guardrails, and documentation that lets engineers ship independently. I work closely with cross-functional teams, translating their needs into platform capabilities and mentoring them on infrastructure best practices — an internal developer relations role that builds trust and productivity through advocacy and education.

I led the company's AI adoption effort — defining guidelines, guardrails, and toolchains (agents, plugins, documentation) for safe and effective AI use across the technology team. This was internal evangelism: building buy-in, demonstrating value through prototypes, and creating the governance that enables adoption without risk. The work required cross-functional collaboration across engineering, product, and leadership — trust-building and pragmatic policy design at the intersection of technology and organizational change.

## Personal development

This role deepened my platform engineering expertise — moving from "I build infra" to "I design the system that enables others to build." I learned to balance reliability with velocity, to advocate for engineering excellence without blocking delivery, and to lead through trust rather than authority. The AI guidelines work pushed me into emerging territory where technical judgment and policy thinking intersect — a skill set I expect to be increasingly valuable.

The platform-building and developer relations dimensions of this role connect directly to a broader career narrative of ecosystem building. Every CI/CD pipeline I designed, every monitoring dashboard I built, every documentation page I wrote lowered the barrier for someone else to ship with confidence. That internal developer relations work — mentoring engineers, translating platform capabilities into team workflows, advocating for developer needs in architecture decisions — is the same muscle as building open-source communities or driving technology adoption in the wider industry. It revealed that my impact compounds when I focus on enabling others rather than delivering solo.

This role marks a clear maturation in my growth trajectory: from individual contributor solving discrete infrastructure problems, to platform architect designing the systems that multiply team output, to organizational catalyst shaping how an entire engineering organization works with technology. Learning to lead through trust rather than authority, to influence without a reporting line, and to drive change through advocacy and evidence rather than mandates — these are the capabilities that distinguish senior technical leadership. The shift from "what can I build?" to "how do I grow the people and systems around me?" represents the most significant personal development of my career.
